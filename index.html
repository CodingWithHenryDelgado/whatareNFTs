What are NFTs?

If you need a TLDR, here it is!

TLDR

NFTs is a Non-Fungible Token. Non-Fungible meaning it has a unique digital identifier that cannot be copied. This unique identifier is in a blockchain making it impossible to hack.

What's a blockchain?

A blockchain is a type of database. Why is this database different from the rest? Here’s this video from Simply Explained explaining how it works.

Blockchain organizes data by splitting it into subsets, referred to as blocks. Then those blocks are chained together, each block in the chain is given an exact timestamp when it is added to the chain. So whenever a block is filled it is set in stone, impossible to reverse or change. If one of the blocks is tampered with then the other blocks deny it.

Blockchain technology was first outlined in 1991 by Stuart Haber and W. Scott Stornetta, two researchers who wanted to implement a system where document timestamps could not be tampered with. But it wasn’t until almost two decades later, with the launch of Bitcoin in January 2009, that blockchain had its first real-world application. As of now, blockchain have yet to be hacked and are more secure than other recording keeping systems!

So what are the negatives?

NFT worth is entirely tied to aesthetic and sentimental value. It’s impossible to gauge its worth as a long-term investment, so right now, it’s nothing but speculation.

Just because someone owns an original NFT doesn’t mean they can control its distribution or duplication across platforms. Ownership just means they hold the authentic “original” — they can’t stop “prints” from being made, screenshots or downloads of the original.

There are also issues with plagiarism and fraud. As some NFTs sellers steal art from artists. One seller posed as Banksy that sold an NFT for $336,000. Which then was refunded after media attention. That was a happy ending but that doesn’t happen with every stolen art.

Also thanks to it’s anonymity that anonymity, especially when paired with a relatively new technology, fosters an environment ripe for money laundering. Someone looking to “clean” dirty money could theoretically generate an anonymous NFT; list it for sale on the blockchain; purchase it from himself/herself from an anonymous, unregulated digital wallet with illicit funds; and then recognize the money as legitimate funds from the sale of the artwork. That’s why sometimes you see on the news some ridiculous image of pixelated rocks sold for a highly insane price, it could be a real millionaire who bought for ownership or money laundering. There’s no way to because of it’s anonymity.

The biggest downside would be how much power usage it takes to make and do transactions with blockchain. A computer is given a problem then it uses sophisticated hardware that solves an extremely complex computational math problem. Once it’s solved the computer gets rewarded, this whole process is called “Mining”. So can any computer mine blockchain/bitcoin? Well if it can handle lots of power throughout the day. Miners usually buy the best computers that have the latest and best graphics cards, power supplies and cooling.

During the pandemic scalpers and miners sought out graphics cards, scalpers sold them at higher prices while miners used them for mining. This caused a shortage of graphics cards making it super hard for those wanting to build new rigs, but execs still predict the supply will remain tight into 2022. If people are mining with one computer, how bad can it be? Well there’s been a pop up of mining farms.

Mining farms is a data center, technically equipped to mine bitcoins or other cryptocurrencies. Mining farms emerged as a result of the constant complication of the mining process, which requires more technical, energy and financial resources. Physically, mining farms are rooms with a large number of computers and servers that take on tasks for mining.One of the main resources into which a miner has to invest is electricity. It is also a risk factor, since the mining farm requires a permanent 24/7 power source. In addition, a large number of processors require an appropriate cooling and ventilation system.

In 2011 a simple computer would mine bitcoin with little electricity. Now it’s a different story. Akten, a digital artist, had analyzed 18,000 NFTs and found that the average NFT has a carbon footprint somewhat lower than Space Cat’s but still equivalent to more than a month’s worth of electricity for a person living in the EU. One researcher calculated that a certain artist selling two pieces of artwork used over 175 MWh, creating the greenhouse gas emissions of 21 years of a U.S. household’s electricity use. There are many articles with all sorts of numbers, the ruth is that The Ethereum blockchain which NFTs use, generates thousands of transactions every day. One transaction uses as much power as the conventional household over a day and a half. As a result, it significantly increases global energy consumption.

There is no doubt that blockchain technology offers secure documents with a transparent transaction. Yet can we ignore its carbon footprint? Bill Gates even expressed concern over the amount of energy consumption. Bitcoin supporters say that estimates of its carbon footprint are overstated. And if the computers that mine and help transact bitcoins are attached to an electric grid that uses wind and solar power, they add, mining and using it will become cleaner over time.

It’s hard to tell if Bitcoin could be mined with renewable energy. As these miners are largely anonymous. Another point experts pointed out is that these renewable energy wouldn’t be available to power a home, a factory or an electric car. Governments that are wary of Bitcoin were to limit mining that would reduce the energy strain. Some miners found a loophole by moving to other places that have cheap electric grids. Though Bitcoin mining might not involve pickaxes and hard hats, it’s not a purely digital abstraction, either: It is connected to the physical world of fossil fuels, power grids and emissions, and to the climate crisis we’re in today. What was imagined as a forward-thinking digital currency has already had real-world ramifications, and those continue to mount.

Tezos is a company working on making clean NFT with drastically lower energy consumption, Tezos is different. The energy used annually by validators of the Tezos network (also known as “bakers” in the Tezos ecosystem) is in the range of 60MWh, a continuous draw of 7 kilowatts. Compared to Ethereum, these numbers differ by a factor of over two million, between six and seven orders of magnitude. Moreover, the low gas costs on Tezos provide for a seamless, congestion-free user experience that allows anyone to mint, host, and trade NFTs with minimal barriers to entry.
