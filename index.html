What are NFTs?

Welcome! You're here because you asked, "What are NFTs?" This website will answer that burning question!

If you need a TLDR, here it is!

TLDR

NFT is a Non-Fungible Token. Non-Fungible meaning it has a unique digital identifier that cannot be copied.1 This unique identifier is on the Ethereum blockchain restricting change and showing public proof of ownership with the buyer.

What's a blockchain?

A blockchain is a type of database. Why is this database different from the rest? Here’s this video from Simply Explained explaining how it works.

Blockchain organizes data by splitting it into subsets, referred to as blocks. Then those blocks are chained together, each block in the chain is given an exact timestamp when it is added to the chain.


So whenever a block is filled it is set in stone, impossible to reverse or change. If one of the blocks is tampered with then the other blocks deny it.

Blockchain technology was first outlined in 1991 by Stuart Haber and W. Scott Stornetta, two researchers who wanted to implement a system where document timestamps could not be tampered with.


But it wasn’t until almost two decades later, with the launch of Bitcoin in January 2009, that blockchain had its first real-world application.2 As of now, blockchain have yet to be hacked and are more secure than other recording keeping systems!

NFTs can be anything, a GIF, a video, music, a 3D house, your medical records, four pixels, etc. You get the point.

1. Immutability

NFTs can’t be changed or replaced in any way if their authenticity is verified on the blockchain. The intrinsic value of authenticity becomes an actual, extrinsic value, as well. 4

2. Smart Contract

Smart contracts — the heart and soul of blockchain technology — make automatic executions after certain events possible. For example, if the NFT owner resells it for a profit later and the artist attached a royalty “rider” to the contract, the artist gets compensated immediately.5

3. New Revenue Stream for Artists

NFTs were partially created to help artists earn more money in a digital landscape that hasn’t been particularly kind to them before. If their artwork accrues more value — which it gains when it becomes popular on the internet — the artist realizes gains income.

For example, CryptoPunk #3100 first sold for $2,127 on July 6, 2017. The collector who owned the artwork refused to sell until March 2021, although whoever it was received plenty of offers.

Nonetheless, that collector is probably doing backflips considering the more than $7.5 million return on investment they earned when they sold.6

4. New Community

NFTs also unlock new possibilities for artists and creators to share commissions, collect resale royalties, host and curate their own creations, and break down barriers created by legacy institutions.

The peer-to-peer relationship between creator and patron through NFTs can help foster new landscapes for engagement and connection for creators, fans, and audiences.7

So what are the negatives?

1. Total Electricty Consumption


The biggest downside would be how much power usage it takes to make and do transactions with blockchain. In 2011 a simple computer would mine bitcoin with little electricity. Now it’s a different story. There are multiple mining farms that mine 24/7.


Akten, a digital artist, had analyzed 18,000 NFTs and found that the average NFT has a carbon footprint somewhat lower than Space Cat’s but still equivalent to more than a month’s worth of electricity for a person living in the EU. 8 One researcher calculated that a certain artist selling two pieces of artwork used over 175 MWh, creating the greenhouse gas emissions of 21 years of a U.S. household’s electricity use. 9


There are many articles with all sorts of numbers, the ruth is that The Ethereum blockchain which NFTs use, generates thousands of transactions every day. 10 One transaction uses as much power as the conventional household over a day and a half. As a result, it significantly increases global energy consumption. 11


2. Shaky Investment


NFT worth is entirely tied to aesthetic and sentimental value. It’s impossible to gauge its worth as a long-term investment, so right now, it’s nothing but speculation. 12


3. Art Repost


Just because someone owns an original NFT doesn’t mean they can control its distribution or duplication across platforms. Ownership just means they hold the authentic “original” — they can’t stop “prints” from being made, screenshots or downloads of the original. 13

4. Art Theft


There are also issues with plagiarism and fraud. As some NFTs sellers steal art from artists. One seller posed as Banksy that sold an NFT for $336,000.14 Which then was refunded after media attention. That was a happy ending but that doesn’t happen with every stolen art.


5. Money Laundering


Also thanks to it’s anonymity that anonymity, especially when paired with a relatively new technology, fosters an environment ripe for money laundering.15


Someone looking to “clean” dirty money could theoretically generate an anonymous NFT; list it for sale on the blockchain; purchase it from himself/herself from an anonymous, unregulated digital wallet with illicit funds; and then recognize the money as legitimate funds from the sale of the artwork. 16


Any solutions?


There is no doubt that blockchain technology offers secure documents with a transparent transaction.

1. Renewable Energy?



It’s hard to tell if Bitcoin could be mined with renewable energy. As these miners are largely anonymous. Another point experts pointed out is that these renewable energy wouldn’t be available to power a home, a factory or an electric car.19


Governments that are wary of Bitcoin were to limit mining that would reduce the energy strain. Some miners found a loophole by moving to other places that have cheap electric grids. 20


Despite accounting for such a minuscule portion of world transactions, Ethereum is already responsible for consuming as much energy as many countries combined and is notorious for putting power plants under heavy strain. If left unchecked, environmentalists have good reason to predict that even clean energy won’t be enough to make such cryptocurrencies sustainable.21


What was imagined as a forward-thinking digital currency has already had real-world ramifications, and those continue to mount.22

TLDR


NFTs are Non-Fungible Tokens that means it’s unique and can’t be replaced with something else.


The reason why it's unique is because the product is thrown into a (blockchain) database that encrypts it with a hash and creates a timeline for the product making it impossible to tamper with the data.


Once purchased you get a smart contract (proof of ownership) of the item's location in the database. The image or product itself can't be stopped from being shared or stolen. The smart contract just shows you are the original owner in the blockchain database.


The energy used to make these and their transcations varies but one example is that one transaction uses as much power as the conventional household over a day and a half.
